01 About
Hi, I'm Anushka.
Here's the honest version.

I grew up watching Bollywood courtroom dramas convinced that law was all passionate arguments and last-minute justice. Then I actually entered the legal world and realised justice is slow, subjective, and sometimes arrives too late for the people who need it most. That did not discourage me. It simply redirected me.

I am someone who gets obsessed with random topics at 2 am, always chasing some personal improvement goal, and genuinely loves long conversations with people who care about what they do. I did not come from a place that handed me connections or a roadmap. Everything I have built has been piece by piece. I am still building.

Professionally, that curiosity landed me at the intersection of law, technology, and business — specifically the systems and workflows that help organisations prevent problems before they become expensive ones. I believe most legal challenges are coordination and information problems wearing a legal costume. That is the part I find genuinely interesting.

05 What I Think About AI and Legal Work
The most interesting question is not whether AI can replace lawyers.

I did not grow up around technology. I come from a small town in West Bengal where people were educated but largely disconnected from it. When I got to Lucknow, 90 percent of my college was headed toward litigation. The seminars held by senior lawyers were mostly arguing against AI in law while quietly trying to figure out how it worked. I was the odd one out actually exploring it — not because it was trendy but because I could see what it made possible.

Most legal work is not about brilliant arguments. It is about information flow, coordination, document processing, and catching problems before they become expensive. Those are not hard legal problems. They are workflow problems. And workflow problems are exactly what AI is built to solve.

What it cannot replace is the judgment layer. That still needs a person. But that person's time should be spent on judgment, not on the infrastructure surrounding it.

Cross-Border Investigation · 2024

Cross-Border IP Enforcement

Three countries. One investigation. No senior lawyer between me and the client.

Maharashtra, India Bangladesh Dubai, UAE

The context

Most legal work happens in offices. This one did not.

During my internship at Maharashtra Enforcement, I worked on an IP enforcement matter that required coordination across three countries. The client was a Dubai-based firm. The enforcement action was based in Maharashtra. A key part of the investigation required an on-site investigator operating in Bangladesh.

My role was the connective layer. I communicated with the on-site investigator, collated field intelligence into structured updates, and reported directly to the Dubai client. No senior lawyer between me and the client.

What the work actually involved

Analysed trademark and counterfeit goods enforcement frameworks under IPR statutes and produced case-specific legal memoranda that shaped active investigation strategy. Drafted statutory complaints, investigation reports, and case documents under strict procedural timelines.

Identified counterfeit sources in Bangladesh through coordinated field intelligence and reported findings directly to the Dubai client, managing all client communications end-to-end.

What I took from it

When you are coordinating across jurisdictions with a client who has real exposure, you figure out quickly what matters: clarity, accuracy, and not dropping a single ball. Most legal problems are fundamentally coordination problems wearing a legal costume.

That is not something you learn in a classroom. It taught me more about stakeholder management and cross-border legal process than any internship confined to a single jurisdiction ever could have.
